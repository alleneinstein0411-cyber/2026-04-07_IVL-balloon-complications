## Abstract

**Background:** Intravascular lithotripsy (IVL) is an innovative treatment for coronary artery calcification (CAC).

**Aims:** This study aimed to compare procedural and clinical outcomes of intracoronary imaging (ICI)-guided percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) versus angiography-guided PCI with IVL in patients with CAC.

**Methods:** A total of 509 patients were analyzed from the BENELUX-IVL registry (May 2019 to September 2024). Angiography-guided PCI with IVL was defined as therapy under solely fluoroscopic guidance. ICI-guided PCI with IVL was defined as PCI with IVL and concomitant use of ICI for procedural guidance either pre- and/or post-IVL. The primary endpoint was procedural success, defined as Thrombolysis In Myocardial Infarction (TIMI) 3 flow, residual stenosis < 30%, and absence of in-hospital major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE). Safety endpoints included procedural complications and MACE up to 2-year follow-up.

**Results:** A total of 537 lesions were treated with IVL: 252 (46.9%) angiography-guided and 285 (53.1%) ICI-guided. The ICI-guided group had more complex lesions, with higher rates of aorto-ostial (20.2% vs. 30.9%; p = 0.005), chronic total occlusions (2.8% vs. 12.3%; p = 0.001), and long-segment lesions (58.7% vs. 69.5%; p = 0.009). Procedural success rates were comparable between groups (88.7% vs. 89.7%; p = 0.71), as were device success (97.9% vs. 97.4%; p = 0.72) and technical success (89.1% vs. 91.5%; p = 0.35). MACE rates remained similar at all time points up to 2-year follow-up (p > 0.05).

**Conclusion:** Procedural success was comparable between angiography-guided and ICI-guided PCI with IVL, despite greater lesion complexity in the ICI-guided group. Complication rates were similar, with no significant differences in MACE or mortality at long-term follow-up.

## 1. Introduction

Coronary artery calcification (CAC) poses a significant challenge in percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI), as extensive calcification can impair balloon expansion, hinder stent deployment, and increase the risk of suboptimal procedural outcomes [1]. The presence of CAC is associated with higher rates of procedural complications, in-stent restenosis, and target lesion revascularization, making lesion preparation a critical step in optimizing PCI success [2, 3].

While intracoronary imaging (ICI) has demonstrated clear benefits in optimizing lesion preparation and stent implantation, it remains underutilized in real-world practice, despite a recent Class IA recommendation in the ESC guidelines [4, 5]. Traditional PCI, which relies on angiographic guidance, provides anatomic visualization of luminal stenosis but lacks detailed information on plaque morphology, calcium burden, and vessel characteristics, all of which are crucial for procedural optimization [6].

To overcome these limitations, ICI techniques such as intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) and optical coherence tomography (OCT) can be used to offer high-resolution visualization of these aspects, enabling more precise lesion assessment and individualized treatment strategies [7, 8]. In comparison with traditional PCI, ICI-guided procedures have been associated with improved stent expansion, reduced restenosis, and better long-term outcomes by enabling lesion-specific treatment [9, 10, 11, 12, 13]. In more complex calcified lesions, ICI plays a crucial role in lesion assessment and preparation, translating into higher procedural success rates and improved long-term outcomes [14].

Intravascular lithotripsy (IVL) has emerged as a novel therapy for CAC, utilizing sonic pressure waves to fracture calcium and facilitate balloon expansion and stent deployment [15]. Although the Disrupt CAD trials have established IVL as a safe and effective treatment, no studies have directly compared angiography-guided versus ICI-guided PCI in combination with IVL [16, 17].

Given the established benefits of ICI in traditional PCI, it is reasonable to expect that ICI-guided PCI with IVL may further optimize procedural and clinical outcomes. However, this has not been systematically investigated. Defining the role of ICI in IVL-guided PCI is essential for refining procedural strategies and optimizing outcomes in patients with CAC.

This study aims to address this knowledge gap by comparing the procedural and clinical outcomes of angiography versus ICI-guided PCI with IVL in a real-world multicenter cohort.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1. Population and Data Collection

This multicenter retrospective study analyzed adult patients (>= 18 years) who underwent PCI with IVL for CAC. Data were obtained from the BENELUX-IVL registry (NCT06577038), an international all-comers registry including patients treated between May 2019 and September 2024. Patients were categorized into two groups based on the use of ICI during PCI.

Angiography-guided IVL was defined as PCI with IVL performed solely under fluoroscopic guidance, while ICI-guided IVL referred to PCI with IVL in which additional guidance from IVUS or OCT was used at any time during the procedure.

All procedures were performed using the Shockwave IVL Coronary System (Shockwave Medical, Santa Clara, California). Technical aspects -- including balloon sizing, number of pulses delivered, inflation pressure, pre- and post-dilatation, and stent placement -- were determined by the operator and documented accordingly.

Demographic, clinical, procedural, and outcome data were extracted from electronic medical records. Patients unable to provide informed consent were excluded. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki, and local ethical committees approved the retrospective analysis of clinical data.

### 2.2. Definitions and Imaging Analysis

All coronary angiograms were evaluated in a centralized core laboratory. The complexity of the coronary artery disease was evaluated using the SYNTAX score algorithm when all three major coronary vessels were visualized [18].

The presence of CAC was assessed both angiographically and/or via ICI when available. Angiographic CAC was categorized into three levels: none/mild, moderate, or severe, based on the fluoroscopic visibility of radiopacities before and during contrast injection. Moderate CAC was defined as radio-opacity observed solely due to cardiac motion before contrast injection. In contrast, severe calcification was identified by radio-opacity visible regardless of cardiac motion, presenting as a distinct line on both sides of the arterial lumen [19].

Long-segment calcification was defined as a lesion length exceeding 20 mm, as assessed by quantitative coronary analysis (QCA) or ICI when available.

QCA was retrospectively performed at two time points -- before and after therapy completion -- to evaluate luminal gain following IVL and subsequent treatment. Measurements were obtained using Medis Suite QCA (2D/3D) software (Medis Suite 4.0.24.4; Medis Medical Imaging System BV, Leiden, The Netherlands) and included minimum lumen diameter (MLD), minimum lumen area (MLA), percentage diameter stenosis (DS), and percentage area stenosis (AS).

IVUS and OCT analyses were conducted using QCU-CMS 4.69 software (Leiden University Medical Center), with additional measurements of reference vessel diameter (RVD), reference vessel area (RVA), MLD, and MLA.

### 2.3. Study Endpoints

The primary endpoint of this study was procedural success after IVL therapy, defined as the achievement of final Thrombolysis In Myocardial Infarction (TIMI) 3 flow and residual stenosis < 30%, as assessed by QCA, in the absence of in-hospital major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), including cardiac death, myocardial infarction, and target vessel revascularization.

MACE rates were calculated based on the number of events relative to the total number of completed follow-ups.

Additionally, device success and technical success were evaluated:
- **Device success:** Successful IVL balloon crossing of the target lesion and administration of therapy without related complications.
- **Technical success:** Achieving final TIMI 3 flow and residual stenosis < 30%, assessed by QCA.

Safety endpoints included the occurrence of procedural complications, MACE, and all-cause mortality up to 2-year follow-up. Complications and MACE were systematically assessed using clinical evaluation and/or angiographic data during hospitalization and follow-up.

### 2.4. Statistical Analysis

Continuous variables were reported as mean +/- standard deviation (SD) or median (interquartile range [IQR], 25th-75th percentile), depending on normality. Normality was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test and visual inspection of histograms. Differences between unpaired continuous variables were evaluated using the independent (unpaired) t-test for normally distributed data and the Mann-Whitney U test for non-normally distributed data. The Kruskal-Wallis test was used for comparisons involving more than two independent groups.

Categorical variables were presented as frequencies and percentages and analyzed using the Chi-square test or Fisher's exact test, with the latter applied when expected cell counts were < 5. The Fisher-Freeman-Halton exact test was used for categorical variables with multiple groups when the expected counts assumption for the Chi-square test was violated.

Kaplan-Meier analysis was performed to estimate cumulative survival free of MACE at 1- and 2-year follow-up, with between-group comparisons assessed using the log-rank test. MACE was defined as cardiovascular death, myocardial infarction, or target lesion revascularization.

All statistical analyses were performed using SPSS Statistics for Windows, version 25.0 (IBM Corp., Armonk, NY, USA). All tests were two-sided, and a p < 0.05 was considered statistically significant.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Baseline Characteristics

A total of 509 patients (537 lesions) were treated with IVL, of which 252 (46.9%) lesions underwent angiography-guided IVL and 285 (53.1%) were treated with ICI-guided IVL. Among the ICI-guided IVL group, IVUS was performed in 198 lesions (69.5%) before IVL and in 213 lesions (74.7%) after IVL, whereas OCT was used in 22 lesions (7.7%) both pre- and post-IVL.

Baseline demographic and clinical characteristics were comparable between groups (Table 1). Patients in the angiography-guided IVL group had a mean age of 73.8 +/- 9.1 years, compared to 72.2 +/- 9.1 years in the ICI-guided IVL group; p = 0.06. Dyslipidemia was significantly more prevalent in the angiography-guided IVL group (n = 149, 62.6%) compared to the ICI-guided IVL group (n = 130, 48.0%); p < 0.001. All other baseline characteristics showed no significant differences (all p > 0.05).

### 3.3. Procedural Outcomes

Pre-PCI QCA values showed comparable MLD (0.96 mm [0.6-1.4] vs. 0.9 mm [0.5-1.4]; p = 0.35) and MLA (0.7 mm^2 [0.3-1.5] vs. 0.7 mm^2 [0.2-1.6]; p = 0.43) (Table 3). DS was similar between groups (68.8% [54.4-80.1] vs. 70.1% [56.4-86.2]; p = 0.08). Similarly, AS was numerically lower in the angiography-guided IVL group (90.2% [78.2-96.0] vs. 91.0% [80.8-97.9]; p = 0.05), but this difference remained borderline significant.

Pre-PCI RVD was significantly smaller in the angiography-guided IVL group (3.1 mm [2.6-3.5]) compared to the ICI-guided IVL group (3.3 mm [2.9-3.8]; p < 0.001).

Post-PCI QCA showed significantly smaller vessel dimensions in the angiography-guided IVL group, with a lower RVD (3.3 +/- 0.55 vs. 3.6 +/- 0.52 mm; p < 0.001), MLD (2.73 +/- 0.60 vs. 3.07 +/- 0.68 mm; p < 0.001), and MLA (6.04 +/- 2.58 vs. 7.45 +/- 2.84 mm^2; p < 0.001). Additionally, post-PCI DS was higher in the angiography-guided IVL group (16.3% [11-24.8] vs. 14.5% [8.1-22.3]; p = 0.05), though at the threshold of significance. Post-PCI AS remained comparable between groups (25.2% [10.4-40.9] vs. 26.8% [15.6-40]; p = 0.35).

### 3.4. Clinical Outcomes

The median follow-up duration was 365 days [IQR: 123-730], with a significantly longer follow-up in the angiography-guided IVL group (n = 238; 510.5 [198.75-730.5] days) compared to the ICI-guided IVL group (n = 271, 365 [92-730.5] days; p < 0.001).

One-year MACE follow-up was completed in 346 (68%) patients, with cumulative 1-year MACE occurring in 11.3% of the overall cohort (n = 39), including 8.2% (n = 15) in the angiography-guided group and 14.7% (n = 24) in the ICI-guided group (p = 0.07).

Two-year MACE follow-up was available for 220 (43.2%) patients. In this period, nine more events occurred (4.1%). MACE rates were similar at the remaining follow-up periods, including 30 days, 6 months, 1 year, and 2 years (Table 5).

Kaplan-Meier survival analysis showed no significant differences in cumulative event-free survival at 1- and 2-year follow-up (log-rank p = 0.18 and p = 0.22, respectively) (Figure 1).

### 3.5. Sub-Analysis of the ICI-Guided IVL Group

The use and timing of ICI varied significantly across hospitals and patients. As shown in Supporting Information S1: Table 1, hospital-specific imaging usage ranged from 0% to 69%, while within the ICI-guided IVL group, the timing of imaging also differed among patients (Supporting Information S1: Table 2).

ICI was performed pre-IVL in 40 lesions (14%), both pre- and post-IVL in 182 lesions (63.9%), and post-IVL in n = 63 (22.1%). Procedural success rates were comparable across these subgroups (n = 32, 80.0% vs. n = 159, 87.4% vs. n = 52, 82.5%; p = 0.32).

Complications were significantly more frequent in the pre-IVL imaging group (n = 8, 20.0%) compared to the pre- and post-IVL (n = 9, 4.9%) and post-IVL (n = 5, 7.9%) groups (p = 0.005). CTOs were also more prevalent in the pre-IVL group with 10 (25.0%) compared to 23 (12.6%) in the pre- and post-IVL group and 2 (3.2%) in the post-IVL group (p = 0.004). Other lesion characteristics were similar across groups.

No significant differences were observed in MACE rates at 1-year (n = 5, 12.5% vs. n = 12, 6.6% vs. n = 7, 11.1%; p = 0.33) and 2-year follow-up (n = 5, 12.5% vs. n = 16, 8.8% vs. n = 7, 11.1%; p = 0.72). An additional four events occurred between the 1-year and 2-year follow-up.

## 4. Discussion

This study compared clinical and procedural outcomes of angiography-guided IVL and ICI-guided IVL in a real-world multicenter cohort. Key findings include:

1. ICI-guided IVL patients had more complex lesions, suggesting a higher baseline risk;
2. Procedural success and complication rates were similar across both groups;
3. Long-term clinical outcomes were comparable.

Despite the greater lesion complexity in the ICI-guided IVL group -- characterized by a higher prevalence of aorto-ostial lesions, CTOs, and long-segment disease -- procedural success rates were comparable between groups. This suggests a tendency among operators to preferentially use ICI in cases perceived as higher risk, potentially to optimize lesion preparation and mitigate procedural challenges.

Although ICI did not demonstrate clear superiority over angiographic guidance in procedural success, the higher, though not statistically significant, complication rate in the ICI-guided group likely reflects the higher baseline risk in this group.

A subanalysis of imaging timing revealed that complications were more frequent in the pre-IVL imaging group, likely reflecting higher lesion complexity rather than an increased risk from imaging itself. Since pre-IVL imaging was selectively used in high-risk lesions, these findings suggest that imaging may have been particularly valuable in this subset of lesions. Without imaging, inadequate lesion preparation and suboptimal stent expansion might have been more frequent, potentially increasing the occurrence of complications and stent underexpansion.

Moreover, the angiography-guided IVL group required more bailout IVL after stenting, suggesting that imaging improves lesion preparation and reduces the need for additional interventions.

ICI-performance changed the treatment that was used; The ICI-guided IVL group used larger IVL balloons, underwent more pre-IVL rotational atherectomy, and had higher post-dilatation rates. Post-dilatation balloon size and stent diameter were also significantly larger. These factors likely contributed to the higher post-procedural MLD, MLA, and luminal gain on QCA.

The significantly larger pre-PCI RVD in the ICI-guided group suggests inherent vessel size differences. However, the comparable IVL balloon-to-RVD ratio indicates that lesions in the ICI-guided group were more proximal.

MACE rates did not significantly differ between groups at any follow-up time point, including 30 days, 6 months, 1 year, and 2 years. Similarly, Kaplan-Meier analysis showed no significant differences in event-free survival between angiography-guided and ICI-guided IVL at 1 and 2 years.

Given the role of ICI in optimizing lesion preparation, a lower MACE rate in the ICI-guided group might have been expected. However, the absence of a significant difference suggests that ICI may have helped to achieve comparable clinical outcomes despite the greater lesion complexity in this group. One possible explanation is the higher baseline risk in the ICI-guided IVL group, which may have offset the benefits of improved lesion preparation.

Additionally, data comes from an ongoing registry, and follow-up is not yet complete, particularly at 2 years. As a result, late events may be underreported, potentially underestimating long-term differences.

These results align with prior studies showing that ICI enhances lesion preparation and stent expansion, particularly in severely calcified lesions [14, 20, 21, 22]. The greater luminal gain observed in the ICI-guided group reinforces its role in procedural optimization [23]. However, this finding may also be influenced by the fact that all CTOs were treated with ICI, inherently leading to maximal luminal gain.

Despite these benefits, comparable procedural success and clinical outcomes suggest that ICI may have mitigated the impact of higher lesion complexity rather than demonstrating clear superiority over angiographic guidance. Given the selective use of ICI in higher-risk lesions, future studies should stratify patients based on lesion complexity to determine whether ICI provides greater benefits in specific high-risk subgroups.

Additionally, randomized trials are needed to establish whether systematic ICI guidance translates into improved long-term clinical outcomes in IVL-treated calcified lesions.

Despite the Class 1A recommendation for ICI in complex coronary lesions, its real-world use remains limited, with significant variation in imaging adoption across hospitals [4, 5]. This disparity highlights the challenges in translating guidelines into routine clinical practice, likely influenced by institutional protocols, resource availability, and potential financial or training constraints. Understanding these barriers is crucial, as broader adoption of ICI could further refine lesion preparation strategies and optimize outcomes in patients undergoing IVL. Future research should focus not only on clinical efficacy but also on practical implementation strategies to enhance the integration of ICI in daily practice.

### 4.1. Limitations

This retrospective study limits the ability to establish causal relationships between procedural strategies and outcomes. The low incidence of complications reduces statistical power, and selection bias may exist as operators prefer using ICI in complex lesions. Incomplete follow-up, due to the ongoing study, may also affect long-term outcome assessment.

While Kaplan-Meier analysis showed no significant differences in event-free survival, the longer follow-up in the angiography-guided IVL group may have affected the detection of late events, potentially underestimating long-term differences.

Variations in operator experience, procedural techniques, and inter-center differences may introduce unmeasured confounders. These limitations highlight the need for caution in interpretation and further validation through prospective randomized trials to assess the clinical impact of ICI-guided IVL.

---

## 5. Conclusion

In this real-world multicenter study, procedural success was comparable between angiography-guided and ICI-guided PCI with IVL, despite ICI being predominantly used in more complex lesions. However, this did not translate into improved MACE or mortality at follow-up. The overall complication rate was low (6.5%), with no significant differences between groups. These findings confirm the feasibility of both strategies but highlight the need for randomized trials to assess the long-term benefits of ICI combined with IVL, particularly in high-risk patients.
