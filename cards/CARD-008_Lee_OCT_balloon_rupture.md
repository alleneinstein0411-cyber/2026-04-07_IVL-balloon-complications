## Case Report Summary

### Patient Demographics & Presentation

- 71-year-old woman [p.2]
- Background: diabetes, dyslipidaemia, essential thrombocytosis [p.2]
- Presentation: non-ST-elevation acute coronary syndrome (NSTE-ACS) with intermittent chest pain at rest [p.2]
- ECG: dynamic changes with deep T-inversions V2-V6 and inferior leads II, III, AVF [p.2]
- 2D Echo: hypokinesia at basal to mid-anteroseptal and anterior walls, EF 53% [p.2]
- hs-cTnT on admission: 28 pg/mL (normal <14 pg/mL) [p.2]

### Lesion Characteristics

- **Culprit vessel:** Left anterior descending (LAD) artery [p.2]
- Severe stenosis at mid-segment [p.2]
- **Tortuous** and **heavily calcified** (calcium visible without contrast opacification) [p.2, p.3 Figure 1C-D]
- Left circumflex artery: chronic total occlusion at mid-segment [p.2]
- Right coronary artery: normal, dominant [p.2]
- Left main: normal [p.2]
- OCT pre-IVL: superficial and concentric calcification throughout the lesion; **protruding calcium nodule along the inner curve** of the tortuous bend at mid-LAD [p.2]

### Procedure Details

- Access: transradial approach [p.2]
- Wires: workhorse wire to distal LAD + workhorse wire to large diagonal branch [p.2]
- **NC balloon test:** 2.5 mm x 15 mm NC balloon failed to fully expand at 18 atm due to heavy calcium burden [p.2]
- Decision rationale for IVL over rotablation: eccentric calcium and tortuosity raised concern about wire bias with rotablation [p.2]

### IVL Balloon Details

- **IVL balloon size:** 3.0 mm x 12 mm [p.2]
- **Inflation pressure:** 4 atm [p.2]
- **Pulse delivery:** 3 sets of 10 pulses at distal, mid, and proximal parts of the lesion (= 30 pulses) [p.2]
- IVL balloon did not open well at the distal segment; planned further 10 pulses at distal segment [p.2]
- **Balloon rupture occurred during the 38th pulse** [p.2]

### Balloon Rupture Event

- **Timing:** during delivery of the 38th pulse (8th pulse of the 4th set, at the distal segment) [p.2]
- **Clinical signs:** patient experienced excruciating chest pain and transient drop in blood pressure [p.2]
- **Fluoroscopic finding:** leaking of contrast from the balloon into the subintimal space, tracking distally (Video 1) [p.3]
- Immediate action: stopped pulsations, deflated balloon [p.3]
- **Result:** Type E (spiral) coronary artery dissection of the LAD [p.3]
  - Visible spiralling of contrast, persistent filling defects, and contrast staining [p.3]
- Post-rupture examination of the IVL balloon: small perforation on the balloon surface with liquid contents leaking out (Video 3) [p.4]

### OCT Findings — Features Predisposing to Balloon Rupture

The authors identified three features on OCT that may have predisposed to IVL balloon rupture [p.5]:

1. **Luminally protruding nodular calcium** — calcium nodule extending intra-luminally at the tortuous segment (Figure 3C) [p.5]
2. **Significant tortuosity of the calcified coronary lesion** — the IVL balloon did not open well at the distal segment (Figure 2C) [p.5]
3. **Possible gaps in contact between the balloon and luminal wall** — inadequate balloon-wall apposition compromises safe lithotripsy energy dispersion [p.5]

The authors state: the combination of these three factors might have led to compromise in the structural integrity of the IVL balloon resulting in its rupture [p.5].

### Type E Dissection Mechanism

- IVL balloon rupture caused release of contrast (liquid contents) from inside the balloon into the vascular adventitia [p.6, Figure 4A]
- The high-pressure leak created a dissection entry point with contrast-filled dissection progressing distally in a spiral pattern [p.3, Figure 4B]
- Persistent contrast staining even after clearance from main vessel lumen = Type E (spiral/extensive) dissection [p.3, Figure 4C]
- **Definition:** Type E coronary artery dissection = spiral luminal filling defects with persistent staining [p.3]

### Management After Dissection

1. Pain relief medications administered [p.3]
2. Angiogram ruled out coronary perforation [p.3]
3. Bedside 2D echo: no pericardial effusion [p.3]
4. OCT deferred at this point due to risk of propagating dissection during forceful contrast injection [p.3]
5. **Stenting:** two drug-eluting stents (DES) deployed:
   - Distal: 2.75 mm x 28 mm DES [p.3]
   - Proximal: 3.0 mm x 28 mm DES [p.3]
6. Post-stenting OCT: stents well expanded; multiple fissures in calcified plaque; large break in vessel intima (dissection) completely sealed behind stent struts (Figure 5B-C) [p.3]
7. Post-dilation: NC 3.0 mm x 12 mm balloon at 18 atm for stent optimization [p.3]
8. Diagonal branch appeared pinched on angiogram but with TIMI 3 flow — deferred further intervention [p.4]
9. Final angiographic result: good [p.4]

### Outcome

- hs-cTnT rise: from 7 pg/mL pre-angioplasty to 89 pg/mL post-angioplasty [p.4]
- Discharged Day 6 on DAPT: Cardiprin 100 mg OD (Aspirin 75 mg + Glyrin 25 mg) + Ticagrelor 90 mg BD, planned for at least 12 months [p.2 Timeline, p.4-5]
- Also given ARB and statin [p.5]
- 3-week follow-up: no angina, improving effort tolerance [p.5]
- LCx CTO intervention deferred given lack of symptoms [p.5]

## Discussion Points — Balloon Rupture Mechanism

### IVL Mechanism of Action

- IVL uses expanding and collapsing vapour bubbles that create short bursts of acoustic pressure waves [p.5]
- Pressure waves deliver approximately 50 atm of pressure into the vessel wall ("karate-chop" style) [p.5]
- Effect is "hard on hard" and "soft on soft" — minimizes soft tissue trauma whilst cracking calcium selectively [p.5]
- **Adequate balloon size is critical** — good contact of expanded balloon with luminal wall is crucial for safe and optimal lithotripsy energy dispersion [p.5]

### Complication Rates in Literature (per authors' discussion)

|--------|---------------------|-----------------|-------|
| DISRUPT CAD III | Not mentioned | 2.6% immediate post-IVL (Type D-F), quickly stented | No mention of balloon rupture [p.6] |
| SING-IVL registry (Ref 5) | Not specified | Low complication rates | [p.6] |
| European Multicentre registry (Ref 6) | Not specified | Low complication rates | [p.6] |
| German multicentre registry, Aksoy et al. (Ref 7) | **9% balloon rupture rate** | Low | Balloon rupture rarely results in dissection or perforation [p.6] |

### Related Published Cases

- Two prior case studies of IVL balloon rupture resulting in significant vessel dissection (Refs 8, 9) [p.6]
- In both cases, IVL balloon rupture occurred in **tortuous vessels** [p.6]
- In both prior cases, **intracoronary imaging was NOT used** for lesion characterization [p.6]

### Authors' Key Conclusion

- IVL is not a fail-safe tool; complications can occur [p.8]
- Specific anatomical features (nodular calcium, tortuosity, balloon-wall gaps) may predispose to balloon rupture [p.8]
- Intravascular imaging should be used to accurately characterize the lesion prior to calcium modification [p.8]
- Not all calcified plaques are the same [p.2, Learning Points]
